# RS2-101: Creating a Theory

## Core thesis

Paper 101 is meta-theoretical scaffolding rather than physics. Peret distinguishes "natural law" (observation) from "man's law" (commandment / theory) and frames RS2 as an explicit *artificial reality* — a postulated description meant to be tested against the natural reality it represents. The paper recapitulates Dewey B. Larson's two foundational postulates verbatim and announces RS2 as the Peret/Nehru re-evaluation begun in 1996.

## Key constructions

- **Natural framework vs. artificial framework.** The natural framework consists of observations expressed as cardinal/scalar magnitudes; the artificial framework is human-imposed, includes the concept of zero, and uses ordinal sequences relative to a "point of consciousness." This separation is foundational to RS2 — every theoretical object is explicitly tagged as belonging to one framework or the other. (Larson's original RS did not formalize this distinction; Peret introduces it.)
- **Discrete unit.** "The minimum quantity you can have of anything is one." There is no zero in nature; subtracting one from one yields negative one (Christian-calendar convention, no Year 0). This is the cardinality basis of RS2's discrete-unit postulate.
- **Cardinal vs. ordinal numbers.** Cardinals belong to the natural framework (magnitudes, no zero); ordinals belong to the artificial framework (a sequence measured from an assumed center / zero). This becomes load-bearing in later papers when projective vs. coordinate geometry is invoked.
- **Reciprocal of content and container.** Change in nature is defined as the reciprocal relation between the content and the container of the Universe. Larson observed that this change is mathematically a reciprocal — the basis for the space/time reciprocity at the heart of RS.
- **Theory as proposed consensus.** "A theory is just an artificial reality that has been proposed for consensus." This frames RS2 as a candidate, not a derivation from prior theory.

## Equations / formal relations

No equations appear in 101. The only formal relation invoked is the *reciprocal* between space and time:

> "space and time were just aspects of speed and possessed the same properties — if space was 3D then time was 3D"

Implicit relation: `motion = space / time` (reciprocal aspects), with the natural-framework consequence that 3D space implies 3D time.

## Verbatim quotes (postulates and definitions)

Larson's First Postulate (observation, "Natural Law"):

> The physical universe is composed of one component, motion, existing in three dimensions, in discrete units, and with two reciprocal aspects, space and time.

Larson's Second Postulate (commandment, "Man's Law"):

> The physical universe conforms to the laws of ordinary, commutative mathematics, its primary magnitudes are absolute, and its geometry, Euclidean.

Discrete unit definition:

> the minimum quantity you can have of anything is one. You cannot possess none of something, nor can you have a part of something, because the part, in itself, constitutes one part.

Maximum quantity:

> you can have more than one, but can never "have it all." [...] The maximum quantity, though very large, is always finite. In the natural framework, there is no infinity.

On change:

> In nature, we can describe change as the relationship between the content and the container.

On the calendar/zero convention:

> Natural quantities work like the Christian calender, where there is no "Year 0." When you subtract a year from 1 AD, you end up at 1 BC. [...] You can never have "none," since, like the Christian calender, there is no concept of zero.

On Newton's observation as motivation for absolute primary magnitudes:

> "any object in motion tends to stay in motion, unless acted upon by a force." Combining Newton's observation with his own observation of speed as the sole component of the Universe, a constant magnitude of speed was required in order for the system to work.

On Larson's stoplight insight:

> the thought hit him that space and time were just aspects of speed and possessed the same properties—if space was 3D then time was 3D and we only observed the net "push" of 3D time on space as a single, scalar variable.
