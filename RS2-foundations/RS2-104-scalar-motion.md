# RS2-104: Scalar Motion

## Core thesis

104 defines "scalar motion" with mathematical precision: the projectively invariant *cross-ratio* of two scalar orientations, with the specific aspects being space and time. The minimum scalar magnitude is unity; there is no zero, no fractional, no negative. Larson's "motion" is a ratio s/t; RS2's "scalar motion" is a cross-ratio (ratio of ratios) where unit speed serves as the natural datum.

## Key constructions

- **Scalar.** A quantity possessing only magnitude. Magnitude is a non-zero, non-negative, non-fractional whole counting number with no specification of "of what." Minimum = unity, maximum = unlimited but finite.
- **Counting numbers.** Three constraints: no zero, no fractional parts, no negative amounts. (Mathematics' real numbers are an "artificial reality," disconnected from nature.)
- **Scalar ratio.** Relation between two similar magnitudes by their relative count. Three orientations: A < B (low), A = B (unit), A > B (high). Ratios A/B < 1, = 1, > 1.
- **Scalar boundary / natural datum.** Unity (A = B, ratio = 1.0) is the unique reference point separating high and low orientations. This is Larson's "natural datum."
- **Scalar orientation.** The three categorical relations (low, unit, high) introduced by ratio.
- **Cross-ratio.** A ratio of ratios — "the only projective invariant in all strata of geometry." Relates two scalar orientations through a constant ratio. Provides the second reference needed to disambiguate A/B from B/A.
- **Dimension (scalar).** A cross-ratio where one orientation is fixed at unity (natural datum) and the other varies. The variable measurement is *relative* to the unity datum. Dimension thus arises as association of two scalar orientations via a cross-ratio.
- **Scalar motion (formal).** "The projectively invariant cross-ratio, with specific aspects of space and time." Two forms: s/t (velocity) and t/s (energy). Larson's books simplify to a plain ratio because unit speed is treated as absolute datum: `(1/n) / (1/1) = 1/n`.
- **Force fields = induced location changes.** Electric, magnetic, gravitational fields are reinterpreted as the apparent location-changes caused by underlying scalar motions. There is "actually no interaction" — fields are independent motions mapped to locations and observed as if interacting. This explains the "instantaneous" character of gravitation.
- **Three scalar dimensions.** Three independent scalar dimensions underlie all structure (astronomical example: galactic expansion as "spots on an inflating balloon").

## Equations / formal relations

The cross-ratio simplification when unity is the absolute datum:

```
(1/n) / (1/1) = 1/n
```

(One-line gloss: when the second ratio of the cross-ratio is unity, the cross-ratio reduces to the first ratio — which is why Larson omits the cross-ratio explicitly.)

Two forms of scalar motion:

```
velocity = s / t
energy   = t / s
```

(Energy is the reciprocal of velocity in RS — this is a defining identification, not a derivation.)

The three scalar orientations:

```
A < B   ⟺   A/B < 1.0   (low orientation)
A = B   ⟺   A/B = 1.0   (unit orientation, natural datum)
A > B   ⟺   A/B > 1.0   (high orientation)
```

Constraint on minimum/maximum scalar values:

```
1 ≤ |scalar| < ∞   (always finite)
```

Definition of scalar magnitude:

> the minimum scalar magnitude is therefore one (unity) and the maximum is unlimited—but not infinite. In nature, all magnitudes are finite values.

Definition of cross-ratio (the central object of the paper):

> A cross-ratio is literally a "ratio of ratios" and is the only projective invariant in all strata of geometry. In a scalar sense, it relates two scalar orientations through a ratio and that ratio remains constant—giving a secondary orientation to the ratios and producing scalar motion.

Definition of scalar dimension:

> A scalar dimension is a cross-ratio where one scalar orientation is fixed at unity (natural datum) and the other varies, in order to make a relative measurement to the natural datum of unity.

Formal definition of scalar motion:

> Scalar Motion, as used in the context of the Reciprocal System of theory, is therefore the projectively invariant cross-ratio, with specific aspects of space and time.

On Larson's simplification:

> In Larson's books and papers, Larson uses motion as a ratio, not a cross-ratio, because he makes an assumption about the absolute nature of unit speed as the natural datum of measurement, and therefore eliminates it from the cross-ratio for convenience, since (1/n) / (1/1) = 1/n.

On force fields:

> What is actually "moving" are locations [...] We call these scalar-induced changes of location, force fields (electric field, magnetism and gravity), even though there is actually no interaction between what is observed.

On the two forms:

> "scalar motion" refers to the ratio of speed, which can be expressed in two forms: s/t (velocity) or t/s (energy).
