# RS2-106: Dimensions and Displacements

## Core thesis

106 defines a "dimension of motion" as a tristate object (unity / speed / energy), shows via Nehru's binary-combination argument that exactly three dimensions are stable, and introduces the *displacement* notation — the difference between an aspect's count and unity — used to label particles and atoms in three orthogonal scalar dimensions.

## Key constructions

- **Dimension of motion (tristate).** Each scalar dimension has three possible states:
  - **Unity** — equal magnitudes of space and time. The "default" / progression of the natural reference system, equal to the speed of light. No energy required.
  - **Speed (s/t)** — temporal magnitude greater than spatial magnitude. Sub-unity motion. Material side.
  - **Energy (t/s)** — spatial magnitude greater than temporal magnitude. Over-unity motion. Cosmic side.
- **Progression of the natural reference system.** The default outward expansion at unit speed (= speed of light). Everything in the universe wants to fly apart at c "just for the heck of it." The progression IS the "tick of the clock" — it does not move relative to itself.
- **Material vs. cosmic sectors.** Speed (s/t, ref. clock time) = material sector. Energy (t/s, ref. clock space) = cosmic sector. Cosmic matter = inverse matter (not "antimatter" by charge, but by inversion of scalar direction).
- **Nehru's three-dimension proof.** For a closed group of operators [1, i, j, k], the unique binary combinations of n basal elements (excluding identity) is n(n-1)/2. Self-sufficiency requires n(n-1)/2 = n. Solutions: n = 0, n = 3, n = ∞. Only n = 3 is finite and non-trivial. Therefore three dimensions is the unique stable dimensional count.
- **Six units of motion in three dimensions.** Each scalar dimension has speed (s/t) and energy (t/s) "offsets" from unity, giving 6 units total. But Larson never references more than three: the 4th unit of material motion is identical to the 3rd unit of cosmic motion (the neutral point sits at three units).
- **Speed ranges (1-x, 2-x, 3-x).** Net motion lies in one of three ranges: 1-x low (conventional reality), 2-x intermediate (white dwarfs, inverse thermal), 3-x ultra-high (pulsars, anti-gravity, dimensional reduction yielding linear jets).
- **Clock as motion.** The clock is the unit-speed datum, not "time" alone. Both clock time and clock space exist. Normalizing a ratio (e.g., 300 miles / 5 hours = 60 mph) reduces one aspect to unity; conventional science routinely drops the unit-denominator, leaving a bare quantity that is actually still a speed.
- **Displacement.** "A measurement of how much something changed from a known point" — that point being unit speed. Notation: temporal displacement n means speed 1/(n+1); spatial displacement (n) means speed (n+1)/1. Spatial displacements written in parentheses, like accountant's negatives.
- **Three-component displacement notation A-B-C.** Three scalar dimensions: principle magnetic rotation, subordinate magnetic rotation, electric rotation. Example: oxygen = 2-2-(2). Atoms with M-prefix are material, C-prefix are cosmic; M*/C* indicates a charged condition.
- **Rotational base.** Subtracted from particle/atom displacements; defined as a "double rotating system" (Larson) / "solid rotation" of a ball (Nehru) — rotation of a 3-ball rather than a disk. Particles have one double-rotating system; atoms have two. (Full treatment deferred to a later paper.)

## Equations / formal relations

Three states of a dimension of motion:

```
unity   : s = t           (s/t = 1, progression)
speed   : t > s           (s/t < 1, material)
energy  : s > t           (s/t > 1, cosmic; equivalently t/s < 1)
```

Force field expression:

```
force field  =  t/s²  =  (t/s) / s  =  energy per unit distance
```

Nehru's dimensional self-sufficiency equation (verbatim):

```
n(n − 1) / 2  =  n
```

Solutions: n ∈ {0, 3, ∞}. Only n = 3 is finite-non-trivial.

Displacement examples:

```
1/3 − 1/1 = 2          (temporal displacement = 2)
3/1 − 1/1 = (2)        (spatial displacement = 2)
```

Atomic notation:

```
oxygen     :  2-2-(2)
fluorine M :  M 2-2-(1)
fluorine C :  C (2)-(2)-1
```

Speed-range notation:

```
1-x : low speed,           1st unit of motion
2-x : intermediate,        2nd unit of motion
3-x : ultra-high,          3rd unit of motion
```

## Verbatim quotes (definitions)

Three states of a dimension:

> 1. Unity, where the magnitudes of the aspects of space and time are equal. This is the "default" condition of a dimension of motion, which Larson refers to as the progression of the natural reference system. Since unit motion equates to the speed of light, the default motion of the universe is outward at unit speed—in other words, everything in the universe wants to fly apart from everything else at the speed of light, just for the heck of it. No energy required.
>
> 2. Speed, where the temporal magnitude is larger than the spatial magnitude, results in motion that is less than unity. [...] Larson uses the ratio of s/t to represent speed [...]
>
> 3. Energy, where the spatial magnitude is larger than the temporal magnitude, results in a motion that is greater than unity. Larson uses the ratio of t/s to represent energy [...]

Nehru's dimensionality argument (quoted verbatim by Peret):

> In a closed group of operators, like [1 i j k], the result of the combination of any number of the basal elements is also a member of the same group. The result of any such combination can be known only if all the possible binary combinations of the elements are first defined in terms of the basal elements i, j and k themselves (besides, of course, the identity operator, 1). Let there be n basal elements (excluding the unit operator 1) in a group. Then the number of unique binary combinations of these elements, in which no element occurs twice, is n(n-1)/2. We can readily see that a group becomes self-sufficient (finite) only if the number of binary combinations of the basal elements is equal to the number of those basal elements themselves, that is:
>
>     n(n−1)/2 = n
>
> The only definite solution for n is 3. (Zero and infinity are other solutions.) Therefore if we regard space (time) as a group of orthogonal rotations, its dimensionality has to be three in order to make it self-sufficient dimensionally. Otherwise the number of dimensions either has to shrink to zero, or proliferate to infinity.

Larson on six units, neutral point at three (footnote citation):

> Inasmuch as the three scalar dimensions are independent, the maximum speed in each is two units, as shown in Figure 8 (Chapter 6). Thus there are six total units of speed (or energy) between the absolute speed zero and the absolute energy zero. It follows that the neutral point is at three units. At any net speed below this level, the motion of an object, as a whole, is in space.

Definition of displacement:

> A displacement is a measurement of how much something changed from a known point. In the Reciprocal System, that "point" is unit speed, the speed of light. [...] Displacements are simply the difference in one aspect of motion from unity.

On the clock as datum:

> The only datum in the Reciprocal System is that of unit speed, from which all motion is measured. [...] This unit speed is what we call the clock—our datum of measurement.

On rotational base:

> For a particle, the A-B-C notation represents motion in three, scalar dimensions, minus the displacement of the rotational base. Larson refers to this as a "double rotating system" that Prof. KVK Nehru later identified as a solid rotation (the rotation of a ball, rather than a disk). Particles have single, double-rotating system whereas atoms have two, double-rotating systems.
