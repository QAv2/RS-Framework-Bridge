# RS2-102: Fundamental Postulates

## Core thesis

Paper 102 traces the historical evolution of Larson's Fundamental Postulates (pre-1959, 1959, 1965, 1979) plus the 1995 *Metaphysical Postulates* added to extend RS to biology and ethics. It then introduces the "Manifest Realm" — the dual sectors (material vs. cosmic) that arise from normalizing either time or space to unity, with atoms re-defined as "little unit-sized chunks of 3D time arranged on a 3D spatial grid."

## Key constructions

- **Pre-1959 postulate set (three postulates).** Larson originally had a third postulate asserting the scalar, magnitude-only, non-geometric nature of space-time, plus three "Laws" (Reaction, Conservation, Probability). All three Laws and the third postulate were later dropped as derivable from the remaining two.
- **1959 → 1965 → 1979 evolution.** "Space-time" → "motion"; "form" → "aspect"; "magnitudes" → "primary magnitudes"; "ordinary mathematics" → "ordinary commutative mathematics." Each shift is a deliberate refinement of language without changing structure.
- **1995 Metaphysical Postulates (four).** A separate axiom layer for biological and ethical existences, treating them as a more general, less restricted, intelligent layer "above" the physical postulates. RS2 inherits this stratification.
- **Levels of Existence.** Inanimate / Biologic / Ethical, framed as discrete units of observation rather than a continuum. Larson refused to reduce one level to another.
- **Material sector / Cosmic sector.** Two simultaneously operating artificial realities: time normalized to unity (3D space + clock time = material sector, our observable side) and space normalized to unity (3D time + clock space = cosmic sector, source of cosmic background radiation). The material sector contains 3D-time "chunks" (atoms) arranged on a 3D spatial grid.
- **Atoms re-cast.** "Little unit-sized chunks of 3D time arranged on a 3D spatial grid." Structure is the arrangement on the grid; atoms are the chunks themselves. This redefinition diverges from the conventional point-particle picture.
- **Entity-relationship diagram.** Paper closes with a diagram (referenced but not legible in the natural-language read) connecting natural/scalar observations to artificial/coordinate commandments via the logic that ties RS together.

## Equations / formal relations

No explicit equations in 102. The structural relation invoked is the *normalization* operation:

- Normalize time → unity ⇒ 3D space + clock time (material sector).
- Normalize space → unity ⇒ 3D time + clock space (cosmic sector).

The same reciprocal `space ↔ time` symmetry from 101 is preserved.

## Verbatim quotes (postulates)

Pre-1959 working postulates:

> I. The physical universe is composed entirely of a single entity: space-time, existing in three dimensions, in discrete units, and in two reciprocal forms: space and time.
>
> II. Space-time conforms to the relations of ordinary mathematics, its magnitudes are absolute and its geometry is Euclidean.
>
> III. (Exact wording has been lost, but concerned the scalar, magnitude-only, non-geometric nature of space-time).

Pre-1959 General Laws:

> 1. Every physical event is accompanied by a reciprocal event equal in magnitude and opposite in space-time direction (General Law of Reaction).
>
> 2. The total amount of space-time displacement cannot be altered by any process within the physical universe (General Law of Conservation).
>
> 3. Where a physical event may have more than one possible result, the proportionate number of each alternative resulting from a number of events of this kind is equal to the mathematical probability (General Probability Law).

Larson on dropping the third postulate:

> "In the early stages of this investigation the scalar nature of space-time was embodied in an additional postulate. Further study indicated that it was a necessary consequence of the previous assumptions, as indicated in the preceding paragraph, and it was therefore eliminated from the list of postulates."

1959 Fundamental Postulates (*The Structure of the Physical Universe*):

> I. The physical universe is composed entirely of one component, space-time, existing in three dimensions, in discrete units, and in two reciprocal forms, space and time.
>
> II. The physical universe conforms to the relations of ordinary mathematics, its magnitudes are absolute and its geometry is Euclidean.

1965 Fundamental Postulates (*New Light on Space and Time*):

> I. The physical universe is composed entirely of one component, space-time, existing in three dimensions, in discrete units, and in two reciprocal forms, space and time.
>
> II. The physical universe conforms to the relations of ordinary commutative mathematics, its magnitudes are absolute and its geometry is Euclidean.

1979 Fundamental Postulates (*Nothing But Motion*):

> I. The physical universe is composed of one component, motion, existing in three dimensions, in discrete units, and with two reciprocal aspects, space and time.
>
> II. The physical universe conforms to the relations of ordinary commutative mathematics, its primary magnitudes are absolute, and its geometry is Euclidean.

1995 Metaphysical Postulates (*Beyond Space and Time*):

> I. There are existences in the metaphysical region of a more general and less restricted type than the units of motion that are the basic constituents of the physical universe.
>
> II. The metaphysical existences are logical, orderly, and rational.
>
> III. Metaphysical existence conforms to a specific set of laws and principles different in some respects from those of the physical universe.
>
> IV. The metaphysical existences of which we have evidence are intelligent.

Larson on the goal of postulates (epigraph):

> "They have not resulted from a search for the absolute truth, whatever that may be, but from an effort to establish a working basis by which the ordinary phenomena of everyday life could be explained qualitatively and quantitatively."

On the manifest realm:

> these little unit-sized chunks of 3D time arranged on a 3D spatial grid. We call the chunks of 3D time, atoms, and the arrangement on the 3D grid, structure.
