# Birotation and the Doubts of Thomas — Nehru 1992

A response to Thomas Kirk's "Dissecting the Birotational Photon" (Reciprocity XX/3 p.14). Kirk accused Nehru of "rewriting the Reciprocal System." Nehru defends by clarifying the load-bearing distinction between **dimensions of motion** (scalar) and **dimensions of extension space** (vector frame).

The paper is structured around what Nehru calls **"the Fallacy of the Incongruous Viewpoints"** — the persistent error of trying to interpret RS's natural-reference scalar motion in the Cartesian extension-space frame, which is inadequate to the task.

---

## §1. The container-space fallacy

> "The viewpoint we have all been addicted to for the past millennia is the one that is germane to the concept of a universe of matter, namely, the inveterate habit of positing every thing as existing in a framework of space and time. We may call this the **viewpoint of Container Space**. With the advent of the Reciprocal System this viewpoint could be seen to be no more valid. Space and time, according to the Reciprocal System, happen to be the **content** of the physical universe."

The container-space frame imagines all motion as happening *in* a pre-existing space-time. RS reverses this: space and time are content, not container.

**For YM mass-gap purposes**: The Yang-Mills mass gap question, as conventionally posed, asks for the lowest excitation in 4D Euclidean SU(N) gauge theory — i.e., it asks the question in the container-space frame. The RS reformulation has to translate this into "the lowest mass quantum in the magnetic 2D rotation of the atomic zone" — a frame the conventional question cannot natively express.

---

## §2. Direction of rotation — the load-bearing claim

Nehru's central technical clarification:

> "When we say 'one-dimensional' rotation we mean that one magnitude (or parameter) is required to fully specify the rotation. A one-dimensional rotation occupies two-dimensional space."

Critical distinction: **dimension of motion ≠ dimension of extension space**.

- A 1D rotation is specified by ONE magnitude: revolutions per unit time (rev/s, or angular velocity in rad/s).
- It *occupies* 2D extension space (the disk swept by the radius), but the motion itself is 1D.

> "From the point of view of motion it is on par with the one-dimensional scalar speed given in, say, cm/sec."

So a gluon, modeled as spin-1 = 1D rotation, has natural angular period = **2π radians** (one full revolution = one cycle of the single-magnitude parameter). This is independent of the fact that the gluon is *embedded* in atomic-zone 2D quaternion structure.

## §3. Why scalar motion appears as birotation, not rotation

> "When the scalar motion is placed in the context of a spatial reference frame it manifests as a **bivector**, and not as a vector."

The bivector argument (with Figure 1, 2, 3 in the original): take a bivector XAXB (two opposing vectors). Decompose along arbitrary axes at angle α and 90+α. Cross-combine components. Result: a new bivector at angle 2α, same magnitude. Since α is arbitrary, the bivector is equivalent to any other bivector of the same magnitude — i.e., **the bivector is tantamount to a scalar**.

The same applies to rotation:

> "All that has been said above of the characteristics of a bivector is also true of the **birotational vector** (or biroctor, if one prefers to call it so). While a rotational speed has a one-dimensional magnitude and a rotational direction, and hence is a (rotational) vector, a birotational vector is a pure scalar."

> "Hence if scalar motion manifests in a reference frame as rotation, it would do so as **a birotation and not rotation**. Manifestation as a rotation would entail the creation of a quantity of angular momentum not existing previously, whereas there is no such need in the case of the birotation."

**This is the algebraic origin of the photon-as-birotation claim**: the photon's underlying motion is scalar (a quantity of motion), and it manifests in the extension frame as **two oppositely directed rotations whose angular momenta cancel**.

The Euler birotation `(e^{ix} + e^{-ix})/2 = cos(x)` is the formal expression: two equal-and-opposite rotations sum to a cosine wave. Net angular momentum = 0; first-order quantities (mv, -mv) cancel; **second-order quantities (mv², m(-v)²) remain additive**.

## §4. Implications for the Yang-Mills mass gap

### 4.1 Gluons are 1D rotations → natural angular period 2π

Per Nehru §2: a 1D rotation is specified by one magnitude (revolutions per unit time). Its natural full-cycle period is 2π radians.

Gluons are spin-1 (per Nehru's "Some Thoughts on Spin" §1: "particles like photons (the bosons), which have integral spin, are based on one-dimensional rotation"). So gluons have 2π natural angular period — *not* 4π steradian period.

This settles the "2π vs 4π" choice for the YM cold derivation: **one full angular period of a pure-YM excitation = 2π**.

### 4.2 Glueballs are bound states; their natural mass quantum still scales with the underlying gluon period

A glueball is a bound state of gluons. The bound state may be JPC = 0++ (scalar, spin-0) — net angular momentum cancels. But the *mass content* of one constituent gluon-like rotation is set by the 2π period, even when the bound-state net spin is zero.

This is the bivector-to-scalar argument: the underlying scalar mass content of one rotational component is independent of whether the net angular momentum sums to zero or not.

### 4.3 The first-order/second-order distinction

Nehru: "First-order quantities (like momenta) cancel each other out (like mv and -mv); second-order quantities remain additive (like mv² and m(-v)²)."

For glueballs: net angular momentum (first-order) cancels in the 0++ ground state, but **mass-energy (second-order, related to mv²)** remains additive. So the glueball mass is the sum of the constituent rotational mass content, even when the spin sums to zero.

This is the algebraic justification for using `ln(2π) × p` as the YM mass gap formula: the 2π is the per-component phase, and the mass content is additive at the energy level even after first-order cancellation.
