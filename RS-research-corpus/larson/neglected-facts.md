## Ch 02 — Multi-Dimensional Motion

URL: `/02-multi-dimensional-motion`

Three-dimensional scalar motion:

> "A scalar motion in one dimension is defined in terms of one magnitude; a scalar motion in three dimensions is defined in terms of three magnitudes."

Independence of scalar dimensions:

> "The motion XB is totally independent of XA, and cannot be combined with it to produce a resultant capable of representation in the reference system, as there is no way of combining independent scalar motions."

Scalar magnitudes can be **summed** but the sum has no direct reference-system manifestation:

> "They can be added. The scalar sum X_A + X_B is a significant quantity for some purposes, but the motion X_B does not enter into any of the physical phenomena."

This is a critical detail for any cold derivation: scalar motions add as scalars, but only one of them is observable in the reference system at a time. The "hidden" scalar motions are present in the dynamics but invisible to standard reference-frame measurement. In modern language: gauge-like internal degrees of freedom.

Electric/magnetic dimensionality:

> "The electric motion as being limited to one scalar dimension...Magnetism is a phenomenon that clearly meets this specification...one dimension of motion is observable in the reference system, and one is unobservable."

Gravity is three-dimensional scalar:
> "Gravitation operates in all three scalar dimensions, explaining force magnitude differences."

## Ch 04 — Speed Limits

URL: `/04-speed-limits`

The **three-speed-regime** structure, important for YM analog:

> "the limiting value of the *total* scalar speed of an object is 3c: three times the speed of light."

> "The three scalar dimensions are independent, and there is nothing to distinguish one from another. It follows that the limiting speed in *each* dimension is the speed of light."

Regimes:
- **1-x** (one-dimensional motion): subluminal
- **2-x** (two-dimensional motion): up to 2c
- **3-x** (three-dimensional motion): up to 3c

Mass interpretation:

> "it is not the charge that enters into the acceleration equation; it is the force aspect of that charge."

Larson argues mass is constant — Einstein's variable-mass interpretation is wrong; what changes is effective force.

Quasar superluminal observation:

> "speeds up to eight or ten times the speed of light"

This breaks Einstein's c-limit because each scalar dimension has its own c-limit independently. With three dimensions × c each, plus possibly multi-step scalar motion, observed apparent speeds up to ~10c are consistent.

## Ch 06 — The General Picture

URL: `/06-the-general-picture`

The **gravitational-progression equilibrium**:

> "at some point, the gravitational limit, we will call it, the two motions reach equality, and the net speed is zero"

Energy = inverse speed:

> "Unit speed and unit energy are equivalent, as the space-time ratio is 1/1 in both cases."

This is the dimensional identity again: at unit speed, energy and speed both equal `1/1` in natural units. Energy = `t/s` in dimensions.

Two-unit scale per dimension:

> "Each scalar dimension spans two units between zero speed and zero energy, yielding a six-unit total across three dimensions with a three-unit midpoint dividing material and cosmic sectors."

Hydrogen Rydberg connection:

> The net speed `1 - 1/n²` is "explicitly recognized as matching hydrogen spectral frequencies."

This is the RS account of the Balmer/Rydberg series — emerges from `1 - 1/n²` as a natural-unit fraction.

Sectoral structure:
- Material sector (space)
- Transition zone
- Cosmic sector (time)
- Time region (sub-unit space)
- Space region (sub-unit time)

Five-region structure, not three. This may be relevant to YM if the mass gap occupies a specific region.
