# *The Universe of Motion* (1984) — RS Volume III

Larson, D.B. *The Universe of Motion*, North Pacific Publishers, Portland OR, 1984.
URL base: `https://reciprocalsystem.org/books/uom/`

Thirty-one chapters. The astrophysics + cosmic-sector volume. Less directly load-bearing for YM than NBM and BPM, but the cosmic-sector framework is where high-energy-regime analogs live in RS — analogous to how QCD asymptotic freedom shows up in high-momentum behavior.

For YM purposes the key chapters are:
- **Ch 1** Introduction — frames the cosmic sector
- **Ch 17** Pulsars — neutron-star analogs
- **Chs 20–25** Quasars — superluminal regime, "speeds 8–10× c"
- **Ch 28** Inter-sector Relations — the mathematical bridge between sectors
- **Ch 30** Cosmology — synthesis

## Ch 01 — Introduction

URL: `/01-introduction`

The cosmic-material cyclic universe:

> "The contents of the sector of the universe in which we live, the *material* sector, originate in a primitive, widely dispersed form, and undergo a process of aggregation into large units. Ultimately the aggregates of maximum size are explosively ejected into an inverse sector of the universe, the *cosmic* sector."

Rejection of stellar fusion:

> "This costly mistake is the conclusion that the energy production process in the stars is the conversion of hydrogen to helium and successively heavier elements."

In RS, stars are powered by an inversion of the cosmic-decay process — atom-building from the cosmic side. This is consistent with the Ch 16 NBM cosmic-atom-building discussion.

> "All of the entities and relations that constitute the theoretical universe of motion are consequences of the fundamental postulates of the system"

No quantitative content in the introduction.

## Cosmic sector → high-energy regime

The cosmic sector is the inverse of the material sector:
- Material: rotational speed < c, vibrational speed > c
- Cosmic: rotational speed > c, vibrational speed < c

For YM analogy: the cosmic sector is the strong-coupling / high-momentum regime. If material-sector physics handles weak/EM at "low energy" (subluminal), cosmic-sector physics handles "high energy" (superluminal regime) where mass and energy interchange.

This mapping is *speculative* — Larson does not write it in YM language — but it's the only place in RS where a high-energy-confined regime distinct from the low-energy regime appears.

## Ch 17 — Pulsars

URL: `/17-pulsars` (not pulled in detail)

Pulsars in RS are not neutron stars in the standard sense. They are products of supernova explosion that have been ejected partly into the cosmic sector. Their masses are given in cosmic-sector terms.

For YM relevance: pulsars are RS's closest analog to "confined dense matter" — but the analogy is loose.

## Chs 20–25 — Quasars

Quasars are the cosmic-sector explosion remnants. Larson asserts redshift behaviors implying superluminal recession, with "speeds up to eight or ten times the speed of light" (per NFoS Ch 4). The cosmic-material boundary at unit speed is repeatedly emphasized.

For YM: this is where "infrared regime" and "asymptotic freedom" might find RS analogs. The 1-x, 2-x, 3-x speed regimes (each independent dimension having its own subluminal regime) is the canonical structure (NFoS Ch 4).

## Ch 30 — Cosmology

URL: `/30-cosmology`

Steady-state cyclic universe:

> "The universe of motion described in this work is a universe of the steady state type...the evolution is a continuing process: a cyclic evolution rather than a linear evolution."

Background radiation:

> "The background radiation is the cosmic equivalent of starlight...It is isotropic because it is emitted by cosmic sector matter that is aggregated in time but dispersed in space."

> "Galaxy motion is inward gravitationally, outward beyond 1.00 redshift in two dimensions"

Larson's "destructive temperature limit" precludes Big Bang singularities. Quantitative cosmic structure parameters are not given in this chapter; UoM is more qualitative than NBM/BPM.

## Quantitative gaps in UoM

WebFetch passes consistently report: no MeV values, no quantitative cosmic mass scales, no conversion formulas in UoM. The volume is largely qualitative.

The exception is in cosmic ray decay (NBM Ch 15) and cosmic atom building (NBM Ch 16) — those numerics live in NBM, not UoM. UoM is the astronomical-phenomenology layer, not the calculational layer.

For YM rederivation, UoM contributes:
1. **The cosmic sector concept** — high-speed regime as the inverse of the low-speed regime, with rotational/vibrational interchange.
2. **The unit-speed boundary** — the dividing line between regimes.
3. **A backbone for the steady-state cyclic structure** — eternal cycling between sectors.

But no equations, no numerical predictions for confined masses, no Yang-Mills analog.

## Recommendation

For Joe's cold YM rederivation: cite UoM only for the cosmic-sector concept (Ch 1, Ch 30). Do NOT use UoM for quantitative content; that lives in NBM Ch 15-17 and BPM Ch 1, 4, 14, 20, 24.
